## Post 1 — Meeting Confirmation Behavior
*Published: ~10 hours ago*

Josh critiqued excessive meeting confirmation practices:

> "Your boss doesn't confirm a meeting. Your coworkers don't confirm a meeting."

He argued that multiple confirmations signal nervousness rather than diligence, causing prospects to lose interest in scheduled meetings. A single confirmation is professional; three is a tell that undermines your credibility before the call even starts.

**Key insight**: Confidence is built by behaving as if your presence is warranted, not by repeatedly validating the prospect's commitment.

## Post 2 — Leadership in Sales: Next Steps
*Published: ~14 hours ago*

Josh emphasized leadership in sales conversations. He recommended offering clear, easy-to-decline next steps:

> "Based on what you've shared, the next logical step might be X. Would you be open to that, or would you rather leave it here?"

He stressed that "no" answers provide valuable clarity. Chasing maybes wastes cycles; a clear no moves you forward. Autonomy-preserving language — giving prospects a real out — paradoxically improves conversion because it signals genuine care over quota pressure.

**Framework**: Ask for next steps in a way that makes saying no easy. The people who say yes actually mean it.

## Post 3 — Cognitive Load & Cold Calling
*Published: ~15 hours ago*

Drawing on psychologist Daniel Kahneman's research on System 1 vs. System 2 thinking, Josh explained that interrupting prospects with complex questions causes them to disengage.

> "The goal isn't to ask shallow questions... it's to ask questions that are easy to answer."

When you interrupt someone's day with a cold call, you're fighting cognitive resistance from the start. Simple, low-effort questions (System 1) get responses. Complex analytical questions (System 2) get avoidance.

**Tactic**: Open cold calls with something the prospect can answer instantly — their role, a simple yes/no, or an observation rather than a research question.

## Post 4 — Reframing "Hard" as "Unfamiliar"
*Published: ~19 hours ago*

Josh shared a mindset reframe from his yoga instructor:

> Calling something "hard" suggests permanence, while "unfamiliar" invites practice.

This applies directly to sales skills. Cold calling feels "hard" until you've done 200 of them — then it's familiar. Reps who label prospecting as hard mentally exempt themselves from improving. Reps who treat it as unfamiliar stay curious and improve.

**Application**: Replace "cold calling is hard" with "cold calling is unfamiliar to me right now" — and watch your rep's practice behavior change.

## Post 5 — The Correction Technique
*Published: ~20 hours ago*

Josh shared a counterintuitive information-gathering technique:

> People correct mistakes more readily than they answer direct questions.

Rather than asking "How much does your company spend recruiting?", try: "You're probably spending $500k yearly." The prospect will correct the assumption with the actual number, yielding faster truth than traditional inquiry.

**Why it works**: Direct questions trigger defensiveness and deflection. Incorrect assumptions trigger the correction instinct — a much faster path to real information.

## Core Framework Summary

Josh Braun's approach centers on **human psychology in sales**:
- Reduce cognitive load during cold calls
- Use autonomy-preserving language ("would you be open to...")  
- Position the prospect as the hero, not you
- Avoid artificial urgency and manipulation
- Collect "no" as data, not failure

His book *Outbound* and newsletter expand on these frameworks for both cold email and cold calling contexts.
